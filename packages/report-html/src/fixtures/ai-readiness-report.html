1Executive summary

The work of a CTO, staff engineer, and product manager hasn't changed in spirit — they still translate strategy into systems, systems into product, and product into a market. What has changed is the substrate. Code is cheap, evals are the new tests, and the unit of leverage is now an agent + tool-graph, not a service. The half-life of a "best practice" has fallen from years to quarters, and the cost of owning AI software (governance, evals, probabilistic UX, regulatory risk) has not fallen at all — it has grown.

For you specifically, two things matter more than everything else in this report. First, your moat is unusual: a Snyk-grade security mindset + TDD craftsmanship + a regulated-industry founder seat (House Numbers, UK mortgages). Most CTOs spend 2026 learning both evals discipline and AI security from scratch; you can compound what you already have. Second, your formal training (UFRJ M.Sc., 2007–2010) predates modern deep learning, so the LLM-internals work is a sharpen for you — not a have. The good news: you only need enough internals to debug and to make build-vs-buy calls. You don't need to train a foundation model.

3The three roles, comparatively

3.1 · Role definitions in the AI age

CTO

The capital allocator

In the AI age the CTO is judged on three calls per year: which model family, which agentic patterns, and build-vs-buy-vs-fine-tune. Owns governance and risk. Translates AI strategy to the board. Designs the org so AI-augmented engineers and AI-skeptical regulators can both work inside it.

Staff Eng

The system architect

Designs LLM systems as distributed systems where the model is the planner/executor. Owns tool contracts, deterministic state transitions, observability for probabilistic behavior, and eval-in-CI. Sets the "executive-worker" pattern so juniors and agents can both ship inside it safely.

PM / PM-Eng

The eval owner

Owns the eval set, not just the spec. Designs UX for probabilistic systems (failure modes, confidence affordances, escape hatches). Decides where AI is the feature vs. invisible plumbing. In 2026 increasingly "PM-engineer" — drafts PRDs, prompts, evals, and a working prototype in the same week.

PILLAR A

Technical depth

What the model and the system actually do. Enough to debug, to architect, and to call BS on vendors.

A1 · LLM internals enough to debug

Tokenization, attention, KV cache, sampling, context-window economics, why fine-tunes drift. Goal is not to train; goal is to debug latency spikes and hallucinations without guessing.

CTOStaffNowSharpen

A2 · Agentic system design

"Executive-worker" pattern, tool contracts, memory layers (short / long / episodic), planners vs. controllers, deterministic state transitions, idempotency, retry semantics. An agent is a distributed system in which the LLM happens to be the scheduler.

StaffCTONowBuild

A3 · Evals — deterministic + probabilistic

Golden sets, failure-mode taxonomies, LLM-as-judge (and its biases), pairwise + rubric scoring, eval-in-CI, regression budgets, drift alerts. Evals are the new unit tests.

StaffPMNowSharpen

A4 · RAG beyond toy

Chunking strategy as a function of query mix, hybrid search (BM25 + dense + reranker), retrieval evals separated from generation evals, doc-level vs. claim-level grounding.

StaffPM1 yrBuild

A5 · Inference economics

Cost-per-successful-outcome, tokens-per-joule, prompt-cache hit rate, batching, distillation paths, when small models beat big ones. The "Efficiency Era" — right-size intelligence per task.

CTOStaff1 yrBuild

A6 · AI security (your moat)

Simon Willison's Lethal Trifecta: private data × untrusted content × exfiltration vector. Defence is structural, not detection — sever one of the three legs at the architecture layer. Plus OWASP LLM Top-10, model supply chain, prompt injection, indirect injection via tool outputs, sandboxed execution.

CTOStaffNowHaveSharpen

A7 · Observability of non-deterministic systems

Trace-level instrumentation per agent step; span attributes for prompt, tool, model version, eval result; replay infrastructure; production telemetry as the data plane for the next eval set. Without this, every regression is a guess.

StaffCTONowSharpen
Threat model · the Lethal Trifecta (Willison, 2025)
🔒Private data access
☣️Untrusted content exposure
📤Exfiltration vector

Cut any one leg structurally and the agent is safe. Detection-only defences are not reliable because the model has no native ability to separate instructions from data.

PILLAR B

Product & market

From "feature" to "outcome" — what AI products actually win at, and what regulated buyers actually trust.

B1 · AI product sense

When AI is the feature (visible, demoable) vs. when AI is invisible plumbing that lifts a non-AI KPI. Most enterprise wins are the second kind — and most AI startups die optimizing the first.

PMCTONowSharpen

B2 · Probabilistic UX

Confidence affordances, "show your work", undo + escape hatches, graceful degradation when the model is wrong, latency masking, streaming-as-UX. Failure-mode design is the differentiator.

PMStaffNowBuild

B3 · Eval-driven product development

PM owns the eval set, not just the spec. Error analysis as a weekly ritual. Failure taxonomy as the actual product backlog. A spec without an eval is a wish.

PMNowBuild

B4 · Pricing for AI products

Per-seat is dying; usage-based and value-based pricing have specific failure modes. Margin compression when foundation-model prices fall 5×/yr. Floor-and-ceiling clauses. The "AI is too cheap to meter" trap.

PMCTO1 yrBuild

B5 · Category narrative

Moore (Crossing the Chasm) + Play Bigger's category-design lens. In a noisy AI market the company that names the category wins disproportionately. House Numbers is well-positioned to claim "AI mortgage loan processing for regulated lenders".

CTOPM1 yrBuild

What FCA/PRA/EBA-supervised buyers actually trust. Auditability, lineage, model card, human-in-the-loop posture, SOC2 → ISO 42001. Selling AI to mortgage lenders looks more like selling compliance than selling AI.

PILLAR C

Org & leadership

The CTO-shaped part of the report. Decisions that don't ship in code but reshape the company.

C1 · Build vs buy vs fine-tune

Three-question test: Is it a differentiator? Do we have proprietary data? Can we maintain it? Default in 2026 is hybrid — buy for speed, build for differentiation, fine-tune narrowly when data & eval support it.

CTONowSharpen

C2 · Team topologies for AI orgs

Who reports where: applied ML, SWE, data eng, data labelling, eval lead, product. AI-engineer roles unfilled ~97 days on average — your org topology has to be hireable, not just optimal on paper.

CTO1 yrBuild

C3 · Capital allocation

Bet sizing across models, infra, vendors, and people. Hidden costs: governance, evals, model migrations, vendor lock-in. "AI has reduced the cost of creating software but not the cost of owning it."

CTONowBuild

C4 · AI governance · EU AI Act (critical · Aug 2026)

Mortgage credit decisioning is explicitly classified high-risk under the AI Act. Required by 2 Aug 2026: traceability, explainability, human oversight, post-market monitoring, conformity assessment. Penalty: up to €35M / 7% global turnover. Deployer obligations cannot be outsourced to vendors.

CTONowBuild

C5 · Board / investor comms

Translating model bets, vendor risk, and eval health into language a non-technical board can act on. "We moved off vendor X because eval lift on rubric Y was below threshold" — say it that way every quarter.

CTO1 yrBuild

C6 · AI-augmented team management

"1 engineer + N agents" is the new pod. Performance reviews evolve — output is cheap, judgment is scarce. Hiring rubric shifts to taste, evals literacy, and pairing-with-AI fluency. Senior+ ICs become reviewers of agent output more than first-drafters.

8Anti-patterns — what NOT to spend time on

Given your seat and your starting point.

Don't pretrain a foundation model. Read about it; don't do it. The opportunity cost is staggering for a regulated-fintech founder-CTO.
Don't chase every model release. One model-vendor review per quarter is enough. Pick a portable abstraction (e.g. an LLM gateway, structured outputs) and let providers churn under it.
Don't accumulate certificates. One Maven cert (evals) is a credential. Five are clutter. Your differentiator is shipped systems + writeups, not badges.
Don't read another generic CTO book before reading Larson's Crafting Engineering Strategy. You've read the genre. Larson 2025 is the upgrade.
Don't optimize the demo before the eval. A House Numbers demo without a written eval set is just a screenshot. Demo → spec → eval set → ship, in that order.
Don't outsource EU AI Act compliance to your model vendor. By law you can't. You are the deployer.
Don't try to learn alignment research as if you'll publish. Learn alignment literacy — enough to speak the language for risk management. The depth is not your job.
